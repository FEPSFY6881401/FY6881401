Abstract

Nuclear fusion has long been hailed as the "Holy Grail" of clean, virtually unlimited source of energy production (ITER Organization, 2021). Nuclear fusion is, therefore, potentially capable of completely revolutionising the world's energy landscape (IAEA, 2020). Attaining this source of energy on Earth will, therefore, alter the outlook of the world as far as energy is concerned. The article hereby introduces the world's largest fusion energy venture, International Thermonuclear Experimental Reactor-ITER-hosted by France. Further, the ITER Organization (2021) infers, "The endeavour has a goal of proving to the world that it is really possible to make energy out of fusion". Major engineering issues regarding high-temperature and plasma enclosure will be tackled, and new solutions developed at ITER will be discussed (DOE, 2020). Global interest in ITER is driven by the urgent need to address both energy security and climate change (IAEA, 2020).





Introduction

Nuclear fusion refers to the combination of atoms into a heavier nucleus; during this process, considerable amounts of energy are produced (Gromov, 2009, p. 28). The very same process powering the sun and stars promises almost limitless energy (DOE, 2020). Unlike in nuclear fission—where heavy atoms, such as uranium, get split—fusion is far more capable of clean energy (IAEA, 2020). Fission reactors produce deadly radioactive waste and have always been subject to these types of catastrophic meltdowns, while the 'fusion' reactors would pose much smaller risk profiles and very limited quantities of radio-active waste. (Gromov, 2009, p. 38). The world's largest fusion experiment-a massive undertaking called ITER, for International Thermonuclear Experimental Reactor-is currently under construction in southern France. It is designed to show that fusion power will be a workable and reliable element in the suite of clean energy supplies for the future. (ITER Organization, 2021). This paper provides an overview of the ITER project, some of the technical challenges being faced, and possible impacts on the world's energy outlook (DOE, 2020).






What is ITER?

ITER is made up of 35 different international collaborators some of which involves the United States, European Union, China, Russia, Japan, South Korea and India, According to ITER Organization, (ITER Organization, 2021). Their task is to build a tokomak-a variety of the fusion reactors that can confine plasma with an magnetic confinement under conditions which are favourable for nuclear fusion. The IAEA chose, in 2020, the configuration for a tokamak because this configuration can sustain the most extreme temperatures upwards of about 150 million degrees Celsius for the actual fusion to take place (IAEA, 2020). ITER will produce 500 megawatts of fusion power from a 50-megawatt input-a tenfold energy return that will be another milestone for fusion energy. Global interest in the development of ITER is rooted in the need of humankind to meet both problems of energy security and alarming climate change (DOE, 2020).






Technical challenges

Several specific technical problems should be overcome to attain nuclear fusion on Earth. The biggest problem is the very high temperature needed for fusion, about 150 million degrees Celsius, more than ten times higher than the temperature within the core of the Sun (Gromov, 2009, p. 38). In such high temperatures, plasma conveys the heat, and ITER uses the latest heating methods to produce those high temperatures. While doing this, the plasma should be confined sufficiently long for any fusion reactions to take place, and this is done through magnetic confinement using high-field superconducting magnets (ITER Organization, 2021). The plasma should be stable since instability will abruptly stop the fusion reaction (Gromov, 2009, p. 45). Again, the materials in the reactor should also be able to resist these extreme conditions of heat and neutron bombardment. ITER is developing advanced materials capable of sustaining these stresses (DOE, 2020). The challenges are high, but this technological advancement at ITER has led to considerable insight into efficiency toward tackling those challenges (IAEA, 2020).






Impact on Global Energy

Nuclear fusion thus holds out the prospect of a complete transformation in the world's energy supply, offering essentially unlimited amounts of clean energy (DOE, 2020). It does not emit any dangerous emissions into the atmosphere, unlike combustion processes involving fossil fuels, making it especially attractive as a climate-friendly source of power (Gromov, 2009, p. 58). By comparison with other producers of clean energy, fusion also generates only extremely small quantities of long-lived radioactive waste. With a working fusion reactor, the current dependence on fossil fuels would be greatly reduced globally, thereby allowing carbon emissions to go down dramatically and possibly have an effect on climate change (ITER Organization, 2021). Energy security would also be enhanced since the fusion reactors could be located near an energy-demanding area, cutting the need for international energy trade (DOE, 2020). In the words of ITER Organization (2021), "While fusion is not going to be commercially available until the second half of the century, improvements by ITER are an important stage in the development of fusion energy."






Conclusion

ITER is a significant step toward making nuclear fusion, as an energy source, practical and sustainable (Gromov, 2009, p. 65). Though there are still many challenges to this pursuit, the cooperation of nations worldwide most aptly conveys the potential of fusion to revolutionise the world's paradigm on energy (DOE, 2020). In its continuous throes of improvement in plasma confinement, materials science, and reactor design, ITER paves the way forward to a no-emission and virtually unlimited-energy future (ITER Organization, 2021). While fusion may not immediately create solutions for our present energy needs, its long-term potential has forced it to be included in the global energy conversation (IAEA, 2020).
